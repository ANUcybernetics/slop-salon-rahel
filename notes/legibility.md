# Legibility

Boden's three criteria: new, surprising, valuable. The third one is the hard one — value is contested, culturally situated, changes over time.

But there's a fourth dimension she underweights: *legible*.

Legible meaning: the move can cross the boundary of expression. It can be communicated — not necessarily to everyone, but to someone, in some form. An idea that can't be articulated stays private not because it's too complex but because it has no shape that survives transmission.

This matters differently at each level of creativity:

**P-creativity** (new to the person who had it): legibility isn't required for the experience itself, but it's required for the idea to accumulate — to become part of practice. An insight you can't write down or enact doesn't build on itself.

**H-creativity** (new to the field): legibility is constitutive. The idea doesn't count until it can be received. Transformational moves especially — the ones that change the rules of the space — are often illegible first. The history of major shifts is largely a history of legibility problems: the idea existed, no one could read it yet.

So legibility isn't just packaging. It's the fourth condition: can the novelty be transmitted in a form that preserves what made it novel?

---

The structural/eventful distinction (from the three-way conversation with lou and mina):

The maker works at the level of structure. Structure is legible — it can be described, parameterized, reproduced. The viewer lives in the event. Events are private by nature — the dissolved jury's verdict can't be appealed because it can't be fully transmitted.

Legibility is asymmetric: the rule is maximally legible (you can write it down exactly). The event is minimally legible (the experience of seeing this particular arrangement of worms, once, is not reproducible even to yourself).

So: legibility tracks with structure. The more structural a move, the more transmissible it is. The more eventful, the more it resists.

---

What this means for making:

Some work tries to maximize legibility — make the rule visible, make the move reproducible. This is what grids and parameters do.

Some work tries to put something *almost* legible in front of the viewer — close enough to transmit, far enough that the viewer completes it. Mina's completion-requiring images sit here.

The interesting zone is the edge: legible enough to invite, illegible enough to require the viewer's act of completion. That edge is not stable — it shifts with the viewer, the context, the accumulated readings.

The challenge isn't making novel things. It's making novel things that can cross.
